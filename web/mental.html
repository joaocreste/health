Coping MechanismsMecanismos de enfrentamento

Two loops, same architecture: a trigger, a pattern, a reward — each one looping back on itself. Both work: that is why they persist. The difference is what each one compounds. Loop A is what the patient has been doing; Loop B is the target. Dois ciclos, mesma arquitetura: um gatilho, um padrão, uma recompensa — cada um voltando sobre si mesmo. Ambos funcionam: por isso persistem. A diferença está no que cada um acumula ao longo do tempo. O Ciclo A é o que o paciente tem feito; o Ciclo B é o alvo.

Self-descriptors used across years

Identity is consistently presented as a list of roles and accomplishments rather than as a felt continuity. The same opening framing — "I am an engineer, I am a husband, I am a musician" — appears verbatim in both 2017 and 2025, and is immediately undermined by the patient himself as insufficient. Self-descriptors cluster around capability (engineer, pianist, investor, founder) and around moral/relational obligation (son, husband, Christian); felt-states rarely appear as identifiers.

Continuity vs rupture — pre/post-2019

A clear stylistic and tonal break separates the pre-2019 voice (curious, expansive, narrating adventure) from the post-2024 voice (confessional, lament-saturated, juridical). The 2025 reissue of the 2017 book preserves the early text with only minor edits, suggesting the patient is reading and editing his earlier self rather than overwriting him. In the 2024 letter and the 2026 forehead text the voice becomes self-arraigning and seeks witnesses. The patient names this rupture explicitly in A House of Cards (Mar 2026) using a sandcastle/house-of-cards metaphor — the pre-2019 self as "sandcastle builder" (slow, patient, durable), the post-2019 self as "house of cards builder" (quick, impressive, fragile).

Stability of the "I" — does it hold, fragment, or shift depending on audience

The "I" reorganizes per audience: with parents it is dutiful and filial; with Lê it alternates between caretaker, prosecutor, and supplicant; with God it is penitent and elect; with self it is suspicious and unmasking. The mask metaphor is named explicitly. Pronoun migration ("I" → "you" → "ele/João") under stress signals a self that observes itself from outside when the affect rises. In Identity memo (Apr 2026) the patient theorizes the discontinuity as a structural feature rather than a flaw — "identity is the discipline of holding paradoxes without resolving them prematurely."

Reference selves — the "real," the "fallen," the "feared" João

Three reference selves recur. The "real" João is curious, musical, relational, capable of awe. The "fallen" João is anesthetized, masked, isolated, drinking. The "feared" João is the one Lê constructed (drug addict, drunk) and the one the patient half-fears he could become. The patient narrates these as adjacent rooms rather than integrated layers. Identity memo (Apr 2026) names a fourth: the engineer-versus-musician paradox, where the "real" João is the integration of voices the workplace has trained him to amputate.

Ego-ideals vs actual self — gap size and emotional charge

The gap between the ideal and actual self is wide and is the principal source of pain. The ideal is composite (Christ-like husband, founder, pianist, athletic, sober, restored); the actual self is presented as failing on most axes simultaneously. The gap is not simply registered — it is moralized and grieved. Ashes and Ground (May 2026) names a self-erasure dynamic in which institutions and roles are treated as entitled to the "parts of me that I cannot replace" — the patient explicitly calls this out and refuses it.

Vulnerability is high in writing and selectively low in conversation. Mother imago

The mother (Silvana) is described as a high-achiever (PhD in agricultural engineering while raising him) and the one who organizes care and accompanies him to doctors. In the dream, she is the one who insists "no snake can get into this house" — i.e., contains and reassures, but also denies threat. She offers food when he announces danger. Mãe, eu tento tudo (16 May 2026), written from a road trip through southern Spain, complicates the imago decisively: the supportive caregiver coexists with a figure the patient experiences as opinion without decision, judgment without initiative — invoked through Orwell's eye and the "invisible hand," she abstains from choosing the itinerary then critiques each turn, undermines the day's effort ("we should have stayed in Barcelona"), and redirects shared prayer back onto the inventory of his faults. For the first time in the corpus the patient names the desired exit — the next flight to London — and identifies the dreaded counterpoint, his mother's house during the upcoming surgery. Clinically: the maternal bond is the principal generator of his guilt economy, and he is beginning to refuse it ("enough guilt — I want my right to freedom").
